Specialized Tools  Prompt  #

For our first portfolio prompt, we were given the following directions: Search the r/specializedtools subreddit ➚, and find two tools. Then, for each tool, we were to write a description, explaining its function and appearance to someone who may have never seen it before. Then, we were to reflect on what techniques we used in each paragraph explanation.

Try reading one paragraph below, then click on its accompanying link (in the sub-bulletpoints) to see if it makes sense. Did it look like what you expected?

Portable High-rise Apartment Moving Elevator Vehicle

  • Imagine if you had to move into a high-rise apartment -- that does not have an elevator -- in a narrow street, how would you go about it? Your best bet is to raise your heavy furniture or other goods with a Portable Vehicle Delivery Elevator. It is hitched and towed with your moving truck. When you arrive at your destination, strong stabilizers are extended along each side of the elevator, and make contact with the road at an acute angle, which helps keep the elevator stable. Then, like a fireman‚Äôs ladder, the backbone of the elevator extends to the window of your apartment. Then, a flat, seat-like car goes up the backbone to your apartment window, where your furniture and goods can be easily moved in. Then, the car goes back down the backbone to pick up more goods. The reverse of this process happens for if or when you move out.
    • Source: "Machine for helping people move in small towns" by fibeep on reddit ➚.
    • Reflection: My approach here for this second specialized tool was to explain it like I would to a friend. Hooking them in with why you would need it. Then some technical description followed by analogies of backbone and firemen‚Äôs ladder. I think the more informal explanation here helps.

Bubble Wrap Making Machine

  • This machine creates bubble wrap from a single roll of double-layer plastic, filling air in equidistant spots, and sealing the wrap with heat to form another roll of bubble bubble wrap. The machine uses two rollers, one where it pulls the double-layer plastic from, and the other where the final product of bubble wrap is stored. The plastic wrap goes through a few rollers, where first air is injected by one of 13 air tubes, and then immediately sealed by another roller that is heated. The rollers are just cylinders that are anchored on one side. It operates by itself until the source plastic is emptied. The machine itself is not in a protected enclosure, and so the plastic is just slotted in, like magnetic tape in a player, or like the tape in a cassette.
    • Source: "This Bubble Wrap Machine" by The_Tech_Monkey on reddit ➚.
    • Reflection: My main strategy in the above explanation is to work through the steps of the process, as well as increase the depth of the explanations in each new sentence. I also used imagery and analogy to describe the machine. Rereading it myself, outloud, I think it makes sense.

Personally, I feel as if my explanation for the Personal Elevator Vehicle is better, as it's easier to relate to a firetruck's ladder system, which most people have seen, one way or another.

Bar Menu Revision  Prompt  #

Using the main principles of Contrast (hierarchy), Repetition (consistency, uniformity), Alignment (and the criticisms of centered alignments), and Proximity (things that are close together are assumed to be related), or CRAPs, we were toold to redesign a daily special menu. Assuming it will be printed in black-and-white.

Revised

This portfolio prompt did not require me to explain my design choices, but I will do so, anyways.

This menu did honestly take me about an hour to fix. In a weird way, it is overwhelming the number of problems there are with it. The first distraction that I had to fix was the hard-to-read center alignment, and the stray bullet points everywhere. I retyped all of the menu's content into Word and fiddled around with it for a while. Revised

In places around our country, making orange juice makes a lot of money. But there is a bad bug inside of the orange trees that are making them sick. It's like cooties, one tree gets it, and then spreads it to the other trees. This is very bad. This could make all the oranges and orange juice go away forever. Some smart people know how to fix this, but it is weird. They have to go inside the orange trees and change them. They have to change the blueprints of the trees to protect them from the cooties. But other people like you and me are scared of the oranges being different. These smart people have only changed the blueprints of other fruits a few times before. We should be happy about protecting the trees from the cooties. But we also have to know about the risks of changing the tree's blueprint.

Workload Report for this assignment (?) 
    I designed the home page of our website, single handedly.  First, I took an
image from the MHV launch meeting, and I used it because I think it looked very
professional and clean.  Because of the white text for the ‚ÄúIn Delaware‚Äù and nav

a Call to Action button, with a ‚ÄúSee Our Community Partners‚Äù button because that

know where to get medical support through their local community partners.  About
70% of small business websites do not have a Call to Action button, which can be
useful for new users [1].
    My next big decision was to include the mission statement, history, and
program report highlights and full program reports on the homepage.  About 50%
of users to a website do not click into another page of a website [2].
This means most users will only see the homepage.  Every time a user has to
click to a new page, they are less likely to see other pages of the website,
even if a website provides a really important product or service they need.  So,
I believe having all this information on the front page (reorganized and
condensed) is beneficial for the user experience.  The mission statement could
have been edited to be less vague, and the history section could be trimmed down
and highlight their accomplishments better.  But I wanted to preserve
Christine‚Äôs writing as is, since that‚Äôs something core and critical to the
website that you would want to work directly with the client to improve it.
    Overall, I think the changes I did on the homepage were substantial and
mostly effective.  I think the photo choices are better.  And had there been
more time, I would have revised all of the writing on the website.  But as it
stands, I think it‚Äôs a large improvement on the original HealthForAll site.
